Chapter 1: Communication Practices
21 essential terms
Enculturation
The process of learning and adopting the values, beliefs, and communication styles of one's own culture.
Ethnocentrism

Perception
How people interpret and understand messages based on their own experiences and background.

A situation where personal interests may interfere with fair and honest communication or decision-making.

How well a communication method (like face-to-face, email, or video call) can carry details, emotions, and understanding.

Being aware of and respectful toward differences in communication styles among cultures.
Ethics
Principles of right and wrong that guide respectful and honest communication.
Knowledge Communication
The sharing of information, skills, and expertise in a way others can understand and use.
Persuasion
The process of influencing someone's beliefs, attitudes, or actions through communication.
Sustainability
Communicating and acting in ways that support long-term balance between people, society, and the environment.
Diversity
The presence of different cultural, social, or personal backgrounds that affect communication styles.
Persuade
To convince someone to believe or do something through communication.
Implement
To put communication plans or strategies into action.
Inclusive Communication Techniques
Ways of communicating that make everyone feel respected and included, regardless of their differences.

Chapter 3: Negotiation & Conflict
20 essential terms
Negotiation
A process in which two or more parties discuss issues to resolve differences and reach an agreement.
Win‚ÄìWin Strategy
A negotiation approach where both parties' needs are met and both benefit from the outcome.
Win‚ÄìLose Strategy
A negotiation strategy where one party gains an advantage at the expense of the other.
Lose‚ÄìWin Strategy
A situation where one party makes too many concessions and the other benefits.
Lose‚ÄìLose Strategy
Both parties fail to reach agreement and walk away dissatisfied.
Compromise
A negotiation option where each party gives up something to reach a middle-ground solution.
Collaboration
A cooperative approach where both parties work together to find a solution that satisfies both.
Competition
A strategy where one party tries to dominate or win over the other.
Accommodation
When one party gives in to the other's needs, often resulting in a win‚Äìlose outcome.

Chapter 5: Leadership & Development
14 essential terms
Transactional Leader
A leader who focuses on tasks, rules, and performance, using rewards and penalties to motivate people.
Transformational Leader
A leader who inspires and motivates team members to achieve more than expected by creating a shared vision and encouraging personal growth.
Performance Criteria
The standards or measures used to judge how well a person or team has completed a task or achieved a goal.
Authentic Leader
A leader who is genuine, honest, and consistent, and leads based on strong values and self-awareness.
Principles of Assessment
The basic rules for fair and effective evaluation, such as fairness, validity, reliability, transparency, and consistency.
Coach
A person who guides, supports, and trains others to improve their skills, confidence, and performance.
RADAR Five-Step Approach
A problem-solving method: Request identification, Ask for details, Develop ideas, Agree on actions, Review and follow-up.
Task-Related Function
Activities that focus on completing the work, such as planning, organizing, giving instructions, and solving problems.
Maintenance-Related Function
Activities that focus on team relationships and morale, such as encouraging, supporting, and resolving conflicts.
Legitimate Power
Power that comes from a person's official position or role, such as a manager or teacher.
Coercive Power
Power based on the ability to punish or apply negative consequences.
Expert Power
Power that comes from special knowledge, skills, or experience.
Reward Power
Power based on the ability to give rewards, such as praise, grades, promotions, or bonuses.
Self-Awareness
The ability to understand your own emotions, strengths, weaknesses, and behavior, and how they affect others.

CASE STUDY - CHAPTER 1

Classroom Miscommunication

Situation:
A teacher gives instructions for a group project. Some students submit posters, while others submit PowerPoint slides. The class is confused about what was required.

Problem:
The message was not clear, and students did not give feedback to confirm understanding.

Question:
Analyze this situation using the communication process model. Identify what went wrong and propose solutions to prevent this miscommunication. Your answer should address the five components of communication and suggest specific improvements.
üí° Sample Answer

This classroom miscommunication demonstrates breakdowns in multiple components of the communication process. First, the sender (teacher) did not deliver the message clearly enough, possibly using vague language. The message itself lacked specificity about the required format. The channel may have been inadequate‚Äîperhaps only verbal instructions without written confirmation. The receivers (students) did not fully process the message, as evidenced by different interpretations. Most critically, there was no feedback loop to verify understanding before students began work.

To prevent this, the teacher should: (1) Provide clear, written instructions alongside verbal explanation, (2) Show a sample of the expected deliverable, (3) Ask students to explain the task in their own words as feedback, (4) Create a checklist of requirements, and (5) Allow time for clarifying questions. Additionally, students should take responsibility by asking questions when unclear and confirming their understanding. This demonstrates that effective communication requires active participation from both sender and receiver, with feedback serving as the critical verification mechanism.

SCENARIO - CHAPTER 3

Workplace Conflict Resolution

Scenario:
You are a team leader at a marketing company. Two of your team members, Sarah and John, both want to take the same day off to attend important personal events. Sarah needs the day for her sister's wedding, and John needs it for his child's graduation ceremony. Both events are on the same date, and you need at least one person present to handle an important client presentation.

Question:
Using your knowledge of negotiation strategies, conflict management, and emotional intelligence, explain how you would resolve this conflict. Your answer should include: (1) the negotiation strategy you would use, (2) how you would apply emotional intelligence, (3) the steps you would take using a conflict map, and (4) the expected outcome.
üí° Sample Answer

To resolve this conflict, I would employ a Win-Win negotiation strategy combined with emotional intelligence and systematic conflict mapping. First, I would meet with both Sarah and John individually to understand their needs and demonstrate empathy‚Äîacknowledging that both events are personally significant. This shows emotional intelligence by recognizing and validating their feelings.

Using a conflict map, I would: (1) Identify the core issue (both need same day off, but coverage is required), (2) List stakeholders (Sarah, John, client, team), (3) Understand each person's needs (Sarah: attend wedding; John: attend graduation; Company: client presentation coverage), and (4) Explore possible solutions.

Through active listening and collaborative problem-solving, I would present several options: (a) Can the client presentation be rescheduled? (b) Can another team member cover? (c) Can one event be attended partially (come in late/leave early)? (d) Can they share the day by splitting coverage? After discussion, we might discover that Sarah's wedding is in the evening, allowing her to handle the morning presentation, while John covers afternoon follow-up remotely.

The expected outcome is a Win-Win solution where both attend their important events, client needs are met, and team relationships remain positive. By using assertive communication, showing empathy, and focusing on collaborative problem-solving rather than positional authority, I would maintain team morale while meeting business objectives. This approach demonstrates that conflict, when managed with emotional intelligence, can strengthen rather than damage professional relationships.
